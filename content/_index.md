<h3>Short History of the College Football Playoff</h3>
<p>The College Football Playoff (CFP) revolutionized the way national champions are crowned in NCAA Division I FBS football. Launched in the 2014-15 season, it replaced the controversial Bowl Championship Series (BCS), which relied on polls and computer rankings to select just two teams for a title game. The initial CFP format featured four teams, selected by a 13-member committee, competing in two semifinals followed by a championship.</p>
<p>The first CFP National Championship in 2015 saw Ohio State defeat Oregon 42-20, marking the beginning of an era dominated by powerhouses like Alabama, Clemson, and Georgia. Alabama, under Nick Saban, won three titles in the four-team era (2016, 2018, 2021), while Clemson claimed two (2017, 2019). The system aimed to create more fairness by including the top four ranked teams, but critics argued it excluded deserving squads from non-power conferences.</p>
<p>Over the years, the playoff expanded its footprint. Semifinal games rotated among six major bowls: Rose, Sugar, Orange, Cotton, Peach, and Fiesta. Viewership soared, with the 2023 championship (Georgia vs. TCU) drawing over 25 million viewers. However, calls for inclusion grew louder after upsets and conference realignments left strong teams on the outside.</p>
<p>In response, the CFP expanded to 12 teams starting with the 2024-25 season (championship in 2025). This format guarantees spots for the five highest-ranked conference champions, plus seven at-large bids, with the top four seeds earning first-round byes. The change was driven by the desire to include more programs, boost revenue, and reflect the evolving landscape of college football amid NIL deals and the transfer portal.</p>
<p>The 2025-26 playoff (culminating in this 2026 game) is the second under the 12-team model. It has already produced thrilling moments, like underdogs advancing deep into the bracket. Indiana's run exemplifies the format's success in giving "Cinderella" stories a shot, while Miami's upsets highlight the parity it promotes. The CFP's history is one of adaptation, from BCS controversies to today's inclusive system, ensuring the sport remains dynamic and fan-engaging.</p>

<h3>Future Growth of the College Football Playoff</h3>
<p>As the CFP evolves, discussions about its future are heating up. The current 12-team format is locked in through the 2025-26 season, but a new ESPN media rights deal runs from 2026-27 through 2031-32, opening the door for changes. A key deadline looms on January 23, 2026, for finalizing the postseason structure beyond this year.</p>
<p>There's strong support for expanding to 16 teams as early as 2026, adding four at-large spots to the current bracket. This would maintain most elements of the 12-team model while including more teams, potentially increasing excitement and revenue. However, it requires consensus among the 10 FBS conferences and Notre Dame. The Big Ten and SEC, as power players, hold significant sway‚ÄîBig Ten commissioner Tony Petitti favors leveraging this for a potential 24-team field in the future, while SEC's Greg Sankey supports 16 but seeks compromises on seeding and automatic qualifiers.</p>
<p>A 24-team expansion is also on the table but couldn't start before 2027 due to logistical needs, such as reworking TV deals and possibly eliminating conference championships. This model might grant four automatic bids to each Power Four conference, two to Group of Five champs, and six at-large spots. Proponents argue it would mirror the NCAA basketball tournament's inclusivity, boosting engagement from more schools.</p>
<p>Challenges include revenue distribution, scheduling conflicts, and player welfare amid longer seasons. The CFP's growth could generate billions more in media rights, but it must balance tradition with modernity. If no agreement is reached, the 12-team format persists, but momentum suggests expansion is inevitable. This game's success could influence decisions, showcasing the benefits of broader access.</p>
